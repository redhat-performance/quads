What’s this QUADS thing? What do I need to do with it?

Nothing at all, QUADS is our backend scheduler and provisioning tool for automating current and future server/network deployments. It is transparent and you’ll never need to see it or worry about it. You can read more about it here or our development blog here.

How do I reserve machines?

Machine reservation requests should be done through the Scale Lab request form. Large allocations are approved by the Scale Council which is a cross-departmental group of Engineering managers who have stake in the Scale Lab efforts.

Where are my machines? When do I get them?

When you’ve been allocated a set of machines you can check the assignments page for their host details and time-frame of operation. At the time of allocation you’ll also receive an email.

Follow the JIRA ticket that you’ve submitted. We set a special QUADS status on your request inside the JIRA ticketing system that will better explain the current state of your request:

  • Pending Review: This is the default for all new requests, and means it hasn’t been reviewed yet during our weekly scheduling review to see where it aligns with future scheduled allocations.
  • Under Review: Your request is being reviewed to find an ample time for scheduling based on your requirements. During this period there may be further questions from us.
  • Approved: We’ve found a time range and machine set allocation for you and it’s pending being entered into the QUADS schedule.
  • Scheduled: Your machine assignment has been approved and scheduled into QUADS and is set to fire off at a specific future date – this will be communicated to you via email (or look at your JIRA ticket).
  • In-Progress: Your assignment has been allocated and is currently running and available, details will also be available on the assignments page.

You can view the current and future schedule allocation in several places before your machines become active if you like. Once they are scheduled (but not necessarily active) they will be visible in any of the mediums below:

  • Scale Lab Monthly Visualization Map
  • Scale Lab Visualization Map for All Months

When your machine allocation begins the machines will be auto-provisioned via our QUADS utility and the assignments page will be updated. After your machine and networks are provisioned automated validation will occur. This can take upwards of 3-6 hours depending on the amount of machines and networks in question.

Once your assignment has been fully validated you’ll receive a final email notification that they are ready along with IPMI and Foreman credentials.

You will usually receive the hostnames of the hardware you’ll be receiving in advance so it’s also a good idea to start working on any configuration management automation to make the most of your time prior to the assignment start time.

NOTE: QUADS operates on UTC time so please convert your requests accordingly. We typically standardize on beginning and ending assignments at 22:00 UTC (6pm EST on Sundays).

What’s the FQDN of my Machines?

You might have noticed we are using shortnames for our bare-metal systems. All RDU2 Scale Lab systems have the following domain name:

 

rdu2.scalelab.redhat.com

 

Therefore if you have a system listed on our wiki named f24-h20-000-r630 then it’s full FQDN will be f24-h20-000-r630.rdu2.scalelab.redhat.com

 

Note that anything without a scalelab.redhat.com somewhere in the domain name would not be systems under our purview. For example somesystem.perf.lab.eng.rdu2.redhat.com is managed by the Perflab team (Tom Tracey and Glenn Baker). Feel free to ask us about them anyway because can still probably find you the right contact or help you but we won’t have direct access or knowledge like we would our own systems.

When do I get my machines?

You should receive your machines the following Monday your assignment starts, we strive to deliver these by 09:30am Eastern Standard time. This usually happens earlier than this but sometimes we run into hardware issues and there can be delays.

You’ll receive an email letting you know when the systems are available and have passed systems/network validation.

I’ve got machines, how do I get into them?

The default root password of your systems will be root / 100yard-

You can use this Ansible playbook to copy public SSH keys to your systems.

You also have access to the out-of-band / IPMI interfaces, to access these simply prepend mgmt- in front of the respective hostname.

https://mgmt-f04-h07-000-r620.rdu2.scalelab.redhat.com

Credentials to out-of-band/IPMI should be in your QUADS email, but it will be the following: quads / rdu2@yourRTTicketNumber where yourRTTicketNumber is the number of your Scale Lab ticket.

Is network traffic isolated?

Yes. We provision an isolated VLAN (q-in-q/Juniper) on the internal interfaces of all assigned machines so your network traffic between hosts is your entirely your own. There will be a “Public Network” and multiple, back-end (10GbE+) “Private Networks.”

We currently offer two VLAN modes and default to using interface isolation for all secondary interfaces and beyond.

qinq: 0

This is the default. qinq VLAN separation by interface: all private network QUADS-managed interfaces all match the same VLAN membership across other hosts in the same cloud allocation.

Each interface per host is in its own VLAN, and these match across the rest of your allocated hosts by interface (all nic1, all nic2, all nic3, all nic4 etc). 

qinq: 1

All QUADS-managed interfaces are in the same qinq VLAN.

I am using OpenStack, what should my instackenv.json file look like?

You’re in luck, we auto-generate and keep an instackenv.json file available for you that will match your current environment. If your machines or environment change this file will be updated. This is found on the assignments summary for your assignment (column to the right).

 

By default all of your systems are listed in the generated instackenv.json, you will need to select which host you want to be your undercloud before you deploy.

 

1) Log into Foreman

2) Select the host you want as your undercloud and click edit.

3) set overcloud: false as a host parameter to have it omitted from the next automated generation of the file.

 

 

After a minute or less your instackenv.json file will be generated again, omitting any host that contains overcloud: false.

Always pull a fresh copy when doing redeploys.

Please do not create your own instackenv.json.

How do I re-provision my machines? (SuperMicro and HP Only)

We offer a Foreman self service interface for users to re-provision their machines if they desire.

 

 

Your login and password will be as follows:

login: cloud0X (your cloud assignment)

pass: rdu2@your-jira-ticket-number (where this is the JIRA ticket number of your assignment)

 

e.g. rdu2@303443

 

To Reprovision:

  • Click on your target host(s) and selection action -> Build Hosts

 

 

  • Reboot the host to boot into kickstart.
  • Note: you can also now reboot your system directly through the Foreman UI
  • With the same host(s) selected, click on Action -> Change Power StateNext, select Power: Reboot and click Submit.

 

 

 

  • Note: This is all that’s required for SuperMicro and HP systems, for Dell you will need to follow the steps below to enact a “one-shot” boot off the Foreman/provisioning interface.

Your server IPMI credentials are quads / rdu2@your-jira-ticket-number for any assigned hosts.

 

Re-Provisioning a Different Operating System

If you want to change the Operating System select another one from the drop-down as needed e.g. Fedora, Rocky, RHEL8/9, note the Media and Partition Table choices as well.

If selecting a different OS than what it is currently set to you’ll need to do this for each system or use a tool like hammer-cli to do it en-masse. You can always ping us on #scalelabrdu IRC or reply to your active cloud assignment ticket or otherwise contact us and we can do this for you if that’s easier.

 

Note: SuperMicro and HP systems that were previously an Undercloud node may need the following run against them to re-enable the persistent PXE flag on their interfaces if you are unable to PXE boot to Foreman to kickstart them.

 

Badfish method

podman run --rm --dns 10.1.32.3 quay.io/quads/badfish -H mgmt-$host -u quads -p rdu2@YOUR_TICKET_NUMBER --pxe

 

Ipmitool method

ipmitool -I lanplus -H mgmt-$host -U quads -P rdu2@YOUR_TICKET_NUMBER chassis bootdev pxe options=persistent

How do I re-provision my machines (DELL ONLY)

 

All systems are provisioned by Foreman via the steps above, however Dells require a second step.

 

Dell systems are setup with a particular interface boot order to accommodate OpenStack Director, in that the primary interface will be one of the internal 10/25/40GbE interfaces and not the interface used for Foreman provisioning.

With RDU2 and QUADS 1.1.x we now have the badfish utility you can use to perform a “one-time” boot. There are two methods for this below.

 

Example: After marking a system for build in Foreman, perform a one-time boot to the Foreman interface to reprovision where 12345 is the JIRA ticket number of your cloud assignment.

 

  • Using podman to run Badfish

 

podman run --rm --dns 10.1.32.3 quads/badfish -H mgmt-f03-h01-000-r620.rdu2.scalelab.redhat.com -u quads -p rdu2@12345 -i config/idrac_interfaces.yml --boot-to-type foreman

 

  • You can use Badfish across lots of hosts with the –host-list command as well.

 

podman run --rm --dns 10.1.32.3 quads/badfish -v /tmp/mgmt-interfaces:/tmp/mgmt-interfaces:Z --host-list /tmp/mgmt-interfaces  -u quads -p rdu2@12345 -i config/idrac_interfaces.yml --boot-to-type foreman

 

  • Run Badfish from Github (see install options here)
    • Requires Python3

 

git clone https://github.com/redhat-performance/badfish && cd badfish
python setup.py build
python setup.py install --prefix ~/.local

badfish -H mgmt-f03-h01-000-r620.rdu2.scalelab.redhat.com -u quads -p rdu2@12345 -i config/idrac_interfaces.yml --boot-to-type foreman

 

  • You can use Badfish to check your interface order also

 

podman run --rm --dns 10.1.32.3 quads/badfish -H mgmt-f03-h01-000-r620.rdu2.scalelab.redhat.com -u quads -p rdu2@12345 --check-boot

 

  • After this you need to reboot the system(s) in order to PXE into a Foreman kickstart.

 

podman run --rm --dns 10.1.32.3 quads/badfish -H mgmt-server.com -u quads -p rdu2@12345 --reboot-only

 

  • You can pass multiple hosts via the –host-list command as well

 

podman run --rm --dns 10.1.32.3 quads/badfish --host-list /tmp/mgmt-interfaces -u quads -p rdu2@12345 --reboot-only

 

Alternatively, you can tell badfish to boot from a specific interface or mac address too.

 

Here are the known Foreman interfaces that you can boot to selectively as needed:

 

 

  • Note: If passing another idrac_interfaces.yml file or providing your own –host-list file you’ll need to map it with -v option if you are using podman or docker and also pass :Z at the end of the first volume declaration

 

podman run -it --rm --dns 10.1.36.2 -v /tmp/cloud09-mgmt:/tmp/cloud09-mgmt:Z quay.io/quads/badfish --host-list /tmp/cloud09-mgmt -u quads -p XXXXXX --check-boot

 

You may also opt to use the iDRAC/OOB interface to manually select the boot interface by hitting F12 when it boots or:

 

  • Open up virtual console / KVM
  • Select Next boot -> BIOS Boot Manager
  • Select either one-shot boot menu or boot interfaces (depending on model)
  • Select the correct interface to boot from

Kickstarting RHEL8 on R660 with UEFI

 

Dell R660’s only support EFI boot in the Scale Lab, in order to change your OS to RHEL8.6 (or latest at the time) you’ll also need to make sure you select GRUB2 UEFI for the PXE Loader category as changing from RHEL9 to RHEL8 will reset this value to PXELinux BIOS which is not compatible with this model.

 

 

My extension request says CANNOT_EXTEND or CAN_EXTEND, what does this mean?

In your cloud JIRA ticket you may see labels applied to your extension requests. These labels such as CANNOT_EXTEND or CAN_EXTEND are fully automated and don’t necessarily mean that we will not extend your assignment, it just means some kind of scheduling conflict was detected. Not to worry, we always look at each request to determine if it is actionable or not so if you happen to receive one of these labels this is why.

In order to manage a very large, rapidly changing bare-metal environment we need to use a high degree of automation and auto-applying labels to designate future operating conditions helps us as a small team to triage and analyze every request better.

I am unable to register my system with RHN, help!

Sometimes previous tenants do not unregister systems before they are subsequently expired, cleaned and assigned to others. If you find yourself unable to register your system please see our documentation here on how to work around this issue. We unfortunately have no way to affect other associates RHN accounts to clean/remove hosts they no longer use. In the future we may be able to get the RHN/CS team to enact an auto-expiration policy for shared lab systems to curb this from happening but right now you’ll need to perform a workaround should you hit this issue.

Can you add Beta, non-GA ISO or Latest RHEL to Foreman?

We’re really sorry but we cannot add beta or non-GA OS to Foreman. As a tenant you have full access to bare-metal OOB/KVM and can install anything you want however (or setup your own internal PXE/image deployment system).

We cannot however add non-GA or non-standard ISO media to the Shared Labs Foreman without validating kickstart templates and functionality and any needed changes across 27+ hardware system types.

Unfortunately this also means even our latest standard version of RHEL may lag behind a few U-releases. See the /root/update-latest-rhel-release.sh tool below on how to upgrade the minor release of RHEL from what arrives on your Scale Lab or ALIAS system.

Note that you can also use /root/update-latest-rhel-release.sh list and if a Beta release is available for your major version you’ll see it as an option.

 

Why isn’t the Latest RHEL the Lab Default?

Similar to the above question, the latest lab-installed RHEL may not always be the latest major version (e.g. RHEL9), while you can still always reprovision your systems via Foreman and choose it (then use /root/update-latest-rhel-release.sh tool to go to the latest minor release).

While we not only have 27+ system types we need to validate major platform releases on, the main reason for this is we need to choose the platform most tenants will use for testing. Right now that happens to still be RHEL8. When this changes to an overwhelming number of tenants who choose to reprovision to RHEL9 first we’ll re-allocate resources to validate and upgrade the default lab OS. This takes time and testing but is also driven by what eventually becomes the most popular preferred baseOS major platform.

There is no cadence or ETA around any specific version upgrades to the new default, it’s a matter of time to validate this (on older hardware too) but also driven by where most products/stacks are being deployed that still utilize the Foreman-provided OS.

Can I Use UEFI? How do I Switch? (Dell Only)

While Foreman provisioning and Scale Lab is set up for BIOS mode you are free to experiment with or use EFI as needed. We realize some workloads or R&D may want this.

You can use the badfish tool to easily toggle between BIOS and UEFI modes.

 

Get Current Boot mode setting

podman run --rm --dns 10.1.32.3 quads/badfish -H mgmt-f03-h01-000-r620.rdu2.scalelab.redhat.com -u quads -p rdu2@12345 --get-bios-attribute --attribute BootMode

Set UEFI mode

podman run --rm --dns 10.1.32.3 quads/badfish -H mgmt-f03-h01-000-r620.rdu2.scalelab.redhat.com -u quads -p rdu2@12345 --set-bios-attribute --attribute BootMode --value Uefi

Set BIOS mode

podman run --rm --dns 10.1.32.3 quads/badfish -H mgmt-f03-h01-000-r620.rdu2.scalelab.redhat.com -u quads -p rdu2@12345 --set-bios-attribute --attribute BootMode --value Bios

 

Please remember to set your systems back to BIOS mode if you can after you are done with them to save trouble for the next tenant as almost all workloads will be using BIOS mode.

Please see the badfish upstream documentation on how to do this for further information.

What Tools and OS Come on Systems

 

By default we ship RHEL8.2 on all systems that tenants receive along with a few tools that are in the /root directory – you will see these in the /etc/motd also.

 

For example, if you want to upgrade from RHEL8.2 to RHEL8.3 or higher you can run the following tool and specify the new major.minor version:

 

/root/update-latest-rhel-release.sh 8.9

 

You can list all available targets for upgrade by using the list command, this is run on a RHEL9.1 system for example.

 

/root/update-latest-rhel-release.sh list
9.2 9.3 9.4-Beta-1

 

Note: RHEL doesn’t support upgrading across major versions without a re-install or using a utility like LEAPP. The update-latest-rhel-release tool is only meant to jump between minor releases of the same major version.

 

You can use the Foreman to reprovision a fresh OS or different one as you prefer however.

What are these 172.x.x.x Interfaces on my machine?

 

We drop down /etc/sysconfig/network-scripts/ifcfg-* configs for all internal VLAN interfaces in order to validate your system and network configuration is setup correctly from our automation. These are also there as a courtesy so you don’t need to spend time creating interface configs if you don’t want to.

 

You can remove or disable these configs at any time via the following tool:

/root/clean-interfaces.sh --nuke

or

/root/clean-interfaces.sh --disable

Conversely, you can re-create those same interfaces per host by running the setup-interfaces tool

/root/setup-interfaces.sh

 

You can find this script in the /root directory of any freshly allocated Scale Lab system or download it here.

 

SuperMicro IPMI/Web Logs me Out! Help!

  • There is a known issue with SuperMicro IPMI/out-of-band interfaces and browser sessions, if you experience this behavior please use an incognito or private browsing tab or window.
  • Unfortunately the vendor is not interested in addressing this though we’ve filed reports and issues about it.

Dell IPMI/iDRAC JAVA Disconnects me! Help!

  • There are known issues and incompatibilities with the JRE on some operating systems with rendering the out-of-band KVM viewer using “native” or “java” – in most cases you can simply switch this to the HTML5 view.
  • Some OOB functionality is only available via Java, on top of that some features only work in Windows reliably (like attaching/detaching virtual media)
    • Here’s a doc on various workarounds to use the native java renderer but below are some specific solutions you can use:
      • Run a Firefox/Fedora container
      • Use the Scale Lab Windows Server VM via RDP

How do I use sol activate for KVM over IPMI on SuperMicro Systems?

 

  • In lieu of using the out-of-band KVM HTML5 console for your servers, you append the following to your kernel line to access the serial console over IPMI. 
console=ttyS0,11520

 

  • It’s been reported that recent versions of RHEL (8.3+) may use a different TTY reference, so try the below if the above doesn’t work.

 

console=ttyS1,115200

 

Note that you can use the following command to test which one is the right one (Thanks to Michal NovÃ¡Äek)

 

/sbin/agetty -L ttyS1 115200 vt100

 

If the TTY is correct you should see the console login.

 

  • Now you can use ipmitool to access the serial console.
ipmitool -I lanplus -U quads -P RTTicketNumber \
-H mgmt-f09-h29-b08-5039ms.rdu2.scalelab.redhat.com sol activate.

How do I request an extension of my assignment?

To request an extension please submit an extension request on the Scale Lab request form.

 

 

Now pick the currently running cloud from the list, enter your full Red Hat email address (or that of the cloud owner) and click submit.

 

Please note that we strive to schedule as many back-to-back future assignments as possible and while it’s sometimes possible to simply extend the expiration date of your machines it can potentially impact and conflict with other Engineering groups schedules so it is not always possible. Your machines or sub-sets of them may belong to several future, back-to-back scheduled assignments for others so in most cases it’s easier to just request a new reservation.

We publish heat map visualizations that better illustrate how machines are being used in past, current and future assignments as well.

I want Machines for a Long Time Where do I go?

Scale Lab is intended for automated, short-term dissemination and reclamation of targeted, high-performance workloads on multi-tenant bare-metal systems/networks. It is not intended for generic testing or just having some machines for a while.

 

The much larger, better staffed PNT DevOps organization within Engineering runs the DSAL lab for these kinds of longer-term, more generic use cases. You can sign up for systems inside their DSAL lab here.

What’s a good use case for Scale Lab?

Scale Lab is for vetting proven workloads at scale, not for general R&D testing or development. You’re much better off using PNT DSAL services or Beaker for general usage. If you’re not sure just ask us, we’re really friendly.

You should also have your deployment methodology/automation working and ready prior to receiving your assignment as we don’t have a lot of wiggle room to extend assignments oftentimes as it may impact multiple Engineering groups who are scheduled after you.
